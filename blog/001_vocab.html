The Problem


Recently, I've been learning German and have found the experience of absorbing new vocabulary fairly frustrating. While German has a steep learning curve when it comes to vocab, I've found that most, if not all, currently available apps lack the thoughtful design needed for effective learning.

Searching for "learn German" right now on the app store presents you with two options: context-based app and repetition-based app.

Context-based apps like Duolingo try to introduce vocabulary in the context of daily speech or following certain themes. While these offer a more engaging experience, I find them very slow, and sometimes the methods used can be distracting (e.g., the mini-games in Duolingo).

On the other hand, flashcard apps like Anki allow users to create flashcards they can use to quickly review large amounts of vocabulary. However, the presentation of vocabulary is completely random and not based on how useful the words actually are. The ability to see and use a word as soon as you learn it is very powerful, in that it not only deepens your memory of that word, but also helps you understand its meaning and its connection to other words, thus strengthening your overall vocabulary skills.

I want something that allows me to learn vocabulary at a steady pace in a way that lets me immediately recognize words in daily conversations or books, without being too slow. The first app that somewhat worked is Linguico, which shows, in flashcard style, German and even some Swiss-German vocabulary sorted by frequency in natural language use. This allows the user to acquire the most useful vocab in the most streamlined way possible. But the downside with this app and many other flashcard apps is that they are completely passive and require the user to manually specify the difficulty of words.

A screenshot from the Linguico app

Above is an example from the vocab training section of Linguico. For every word, the user has to classify it as hard, medium, or easy. Hard words are then repeated many times within a session and will appear more often in later review sessions. Medium and easy words progressively have fewer repetitions and are also less likely to show up in later review sessions. This is a flawed mechanism because: 1. It is very passive 2. Users can't make accurate judgments about how difficult a word is 3. It's very punishing if the user makes a wrong assessment.

So, what can we learn from this?


My Solution


Here are the principles that guide my solution:

1. Leverage spaced repetition
2. Show words based on their frequency of use
3. Present words in context with example sentences
4. Require active input from the user
5. Personalize the review experience
6. Eliminate manual difficulty ranking
7. Create a tight feedback loop (Success Is Not About Trying Hard)

Of these principles, the seventh is the most subtle yet critical. Context-based apps are slow at teaching vocabulary because their feedback loops are fragmented across different modes and exercises. Even within each exercise, the number of words shown is very limited. Similarly, Linguico's constant self-assessment prompts break the learning flow and prevent users from having more practice trials.

So, I've created a flashcard app that displays English words with example sentences showing their usage in context. Users must type out the German translation. Words are prioritized using a ranking algorithm that combines three factors: the word's usefulness, its frequency in natural language, and the severity of any previous mistakes the user made with that word.
